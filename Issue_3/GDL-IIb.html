NICE guidelines revise treatment recommendations for schizophrenia
by Medeka Health | November 01, 2013
Recommendations
Antipsychotics are the mainstay of treatment for schizophrenia. They are used for the treatment of acute episodes, for relapse prevention, and for symptom reduction. A combination of antipsychotics and other classes of drugs, such as anticonvulsants, mood stabilizers, anticholinergics, antidepressants, and benzodiazepines may also be used. Antipsychotics are available as oral, intramuscular (IM), and intravenous (IV) preparations, or as medium- or long-acting depot IM preparations.
  • Initiation of treatment (first episode)
  • Use of oral antipsychotic medication
    • Recommendations suggested for the use of oral antipsychotics are:
    1. Perform an electrocardiogram (ECG) before starting antipsychotic medication if indicated. Conditions requesting an ECG include:
      1. A physical examination identifying specific cardiovascular risk (such as diagnosis of high blood pressure)
      2. Personal history of cardiovascular disease
      3. If inpatient hospitalization is considered
    2. Initiation of antipsychotic medication should be considered on an individual patient basis.
      1. Record the indications, benefits, and risks of oral antipsychotics, and expected time for a change in therapy
      2. Start the treatment with a lower dose and slowly titrate upwards within the indicated dose range
      3. If dosages are used outside the indicated range then justify and record reasons
      4. Regular monitoring and record maintenance should be maintained especially during dose titration:
        1. Efficacy, along with changes in symptoms and behavior
        2. Side effects of treatment
        3. Adherence pattern
        4. Physical health
      5. Record the rationale for continuing, changing or stopping medication, and the effects of such changes
      6. A medication at optimum dosage should be tried for a duration of 4–6 weeks
    3. If nonprescribed therapies are to be used (along with complementary therapies), then discuss with patients and carers the benefit and risk of such therapies and possible interference with the therapeutic effects of prescribed medication and psychological treatments.
    4. Patients and carers should be given clear explanation about the use of alcohol, tobacco, prescription and nonprescription medications, and illicit drugs.
    5. Loading dose of antipsychotic medication should be avoided (often referred to as ‘rapid neuroleptization’).
    6. Combination of antipsychotic medications should be recommended only for shorter periods.
    7. If chlorpromazine is prescribed, warn patients of its potential to cause skin photosensitivity. Advise using sunscreen if necessary.
