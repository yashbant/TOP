Rising burden of MetS in Indians with bipolar disorder
by Medeka Health | November 01, 2013
Metabolic syndrome (MetS) is considered to be a significant problem in both psychiatry and public health. It is a combination of metabolic factors (atherogenic dyslipidemia, elevated blood pressure and glucose, prothrombotic and proinflammatory states) that, when associated together, increase the risk of cardiovascular disease and diabetes by 2-folds and 5-folds respectively.1 The prevalence of MetS is increasing globally and varies with sex, age, and ethnicity.2 In the West, the prevalence of MetS ranges from 16.7 to 67% in patients with bipolar disorder (BD). Further, these rates are higher in patients treated with atypical antipsychotics. Other factors contributing to MetS could be the higher activation of the stress pathway through the hypothalamic–pituitary axis and genetic factors. Very few studies from India have evaluated the burden of MetS in patients with BD.3

Prevalence of metabolic syndrome is high in Indians with bipolar disorder

  • Malhotra et al. study
    • A prospective, observational study assessed the prevalence of MetS in 161 patients with either BD (n=97) or schizophrenia (n=64). Mean duration of illness in BD patients was 122 months and most of them were on either lithium or valproate. All patients were assessed for metabolic profiles at baseline and at 3 and 6 months respectively. Prevalence of MetS in BD patients increased from 40 to 48% over the 6 months. Increase in the prevalence of MetS in BD patients was over 4% in 3 months and 8% in 6 months(Table 1).

    The prevalence rate was lower when patients with preexisting hypertension and diabetes were excluded (Table 1).

    Abnormal waist circumference contributed to a higher prevalence of MetS. Further, the risk of MetS was higher in BD patients with partial MetS (those who fulfilled 1 or 2 criteria of MetS; Table 1).

    Hence, early screening, regular monitoring, and early institution of preventive measures may prevent the occurrence of MetS, especially in BD patients with partial MetS.4
  • Grover et al. study
    • A total of 200 patients with BD were used to assess the prevalence of MetS. Patients were diagnosed to have MetS using International Diabetes Federation (IDF) and modified National Cholesterol Education Program Adult Treatment Panel III (NCEP ATPIII) criteria. Most of the patients were on mood stabilizers (either lithium [n=94] or valproate [n=86]) and more than half of them were also on antipsychotic medication (olanzapine, n=70).

    Out of 200, 80 (40%) patients fulfilled the IDF criteria, and 82 (41%) patients fulfilled the NCEP ATP-III criteria for MetS. Among the various components of MetS, increased waist circumference (70.1%) was the most common abnormality seen. This was followed by increased blood pressure (44.5%), increased triglyceride levels (42%), and low high density lipoprotein levels (41.5%). Prevalence of MetS did not vary between males and females. Patients with MetS were mostly older, had longer duration of illness, and also had higher body mass index (BMI). Patients receiving 2 mood stabilizers were at a higher risk of MetS than those receiving 1 or none. Prediction analysis found that age >35 years, and BMI >25 could predict MetS.5
