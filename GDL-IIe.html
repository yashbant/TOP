NICE guidelines revise treatment recommendations for schizophrenia
by Medeka | November 01, 2013
Recommendations
Antipsychotics are the mainstay of treatment for schizophrenia. They are used for the treatment of acute episodes, for relapse prevention, and for symptom reduction. A combination of antipsychotics and other classes of drugs, such as anticonvulsants, mood stabilizers, anticholinergics, antidepressants, and benzodiazepines may also be used. Antipsychotics are available as oral, intramuscular (IM), and intravenous (IV) preparations, or as medium- or long-acting depot IM preparations.

  • Treatment-resistant schizophrenia
    • Various interventions can be considered in patients with schizophrenia who have inadequate or no response to pharmacological or psychological treatment:
    1. Review the diagnosis
    2. Monitor adherence to antipsychotic medication; whether taken in adequate dose and for correct duration
    3. If psychological treatments are prescribed, review if the patient adheres to the treatment or not and if the treatment techniques are according to the prescribed guidelines or not. Suggest cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) as an alternative for people who were advised family intervention. If family intervention was advised, then prefer CBT especially in patients who are in close contact with their families
    4. Search for any other causes of nonresponse, such as comorbid substance misuse (including alcohol), comedication, and physical illness
    5. Offer clozapine in patients whose illness has not responded adequately even after administering adequate doses of at least 2 different antipsychotic drugs, 1 of them being a nonclozapine second-generation antipsychotic.
    6. If a patient is not responding adequately to an adequate dose of clozapine, then consider revisiting points I–IV. Then add a second antipsychotic to augment treatment with clozapine. The augmentation therapy may need up to 8–10 weeks. The second antipsychotic selected should not compound the common side effects of clozapine.


    Clinical evidence

    Summary from 18 RCTs found that clozapine was the more effective drug than first-generation antipsychotics in patients with treatment-resistant schizophrenia. Among second-generation antipsychotics, more evidences are necessary to establish equivalence between clozapine and any other second-generation antipsychotic. Further, limited data are available to establish differences between any other antipsychotic drugs. Among 10 RCTs examining the efficacy of antipsychotics for negative symptoms, no RCT reported significant clinical differences between the different antipsychotics assessed. However, recommendations suggest that careful clinical assessment is necessary to identify reasons for resistance such as drug-induced parkinsonism, depressive features or certain positive symptoms. Evidence from 6 RCTs (n=252) showed that clozapine augmentation with another antipsychotic might improve both total and negative symptoms if administered for an adequate duration.
