Normalization of reward related activities in schizophrenia - Clinical improvements with Amisulpiride
by Medeka Health | February 01, 2014
Role of amisulpride in improving reward-related brain activations

Amisulpride is a unique atypical antipsychotic that specifically blocks dopamine D2 and D3 receptors. A pre-clinical study demonstrated that amisulpride improves brain reward deficits induced by dopamine agonists. In this context, Nielsen et al demonstrated that amisulpride may lead to improvement in reward-related signaling in the ventral striatum (VS), which may correlate to symptomatic improvement. They conducted a prospective longitudinal study to examine the changes in reward-related brain activations in schizophrenia before and after antipsychotic monotherapy with amisulpride.

A total of 23 treatment-naïve patients with schizophrenia (age: 18–45 years) and 24 healthy controls were enrolled. Patients were treated with amisulpride (100–800 mg/day) for 6 weeks. Controls were followed up without treatment. A modified variant of monetary incentive delay task was used to activate the ventral striatum. Clinical measures of psychopathology were evaluated at baseline and at the end of treatment. In addition, functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) was performed to estimate task-related blood oxygen level–dependent responses. Findings of the study are discussed below:

Effect on symptoms of schizophrenia

A significant improvement was noted in total PANSS (Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale) score, PANSS positive score, and PANSS general score at the end of treatment (p < 0.001 for all, Table 1).

Further, CDS (Calgary Depression Scale) and GAF (Global Assessment of Functioning) scores also improved in patients with schizophrenia (p < 0.001 for both, Table 1).

Effect on behavioral data during monetary incentive delay task
  • Both amisulpride and control groups showed improvement in reaction time from baseline to follow-up.
  • Patients were found to be slower than healthy controls, at baseline and at follow-up.
  • Patients displayed a nonsignificant increase in the amount of money won in the reward task as compared to healthy controls.
