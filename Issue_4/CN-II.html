Association between childhood infection and schizophrenia

Findings of a study presented at the 14th International Congress on Schizophrenia Research (ICOSR) reported that hospitalization for infection during childhood may be associated with an increased risk of schizophrenia in adolescence and young adulthood.

Various studies have reported a correlation between maternal infections during pregnancy and a risk of schizophrenia in the offspring. To determine the association between childhood infection and risk of schizophrenia, researchers examined data from two population-based registers, the Danish Psychiatric Central Register and the Danish National Hospital Register. They identified 3,409 individuals who had been diagnosed with schizophrenia. Among them, 1,549 patients were hospitalized during their childhood as they were exposed to an infection. Researchers reported that:

  • Hospitalization due to any infection during childhood increased the risk of schizophrenia by nearly 50% (relative risk [RR]: 1.45; 95% confidence interval [CI]: 1.35–1.55)
  • Bacterial infection increased the risk by 73% (RR: 1.73; 95% CI: 1.55–1.92)
  • Viral infections increased the risk by 40% (RR: 1.40; 95% CI: 1.25–1.56).
  • Individuals with liver infection during childhood were at a very high risk of schizophrenia (RR: 4.05; 95% CI: 1.45–8.71).

Reference

Nielsen P. Childhood Infection Linked to Schizophrenia. 14th International Congress on Schizophrenia Research (ICOSR). April 21–25, 2013; Florida, USA. Abstract S73.
