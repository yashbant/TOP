Options for switching antipsychotic drugs in schizophrenia
by Medeka Health | February 01, 2014
Amisulpride vs risperidone as a switch option

Risperidone, another atypical antipsychotic, is a central 5-HT2 and dopamine D2-receptor antagonist. A study by Sechter et al comparing the 2 drugs reported better response rates at the end of the study with amisulpride than with risperidone, in terms of improvement in PANSS score, BPRS total score, and CGI improvement score. The functional improvement in the amisulpride group was also better when compared to the risperidone group. Amisulpride was a much more safer treatment option in terms of specific endocrinal and sexual disorders as well as changes in body weight caused by risperidone.8 Another study by Hwang et al, comparing amisulpride and risperidone, reported statistically significant weight gain with the latter drug. Additionally, amisulpride was associated with a decrease in blood pressure and heart rate.9

Risperidone is ranked highest amongst atypical antipsychotics in the hierarchy of elevating prolactin levels. It is also associated with a decrease in bone mineral density, and long-term use (more than 8 years) in premenstrual women has been associated with osteoporosis or osteopenia.10 Studies have shown higher success rates on switching to amisulpride when compared to risperidone. The switch to amisulpride was easy in most cases (87%) and showed improvements in terms of efficacy, tolerability, and compliance.6 On the contrary, only 64% patients exhibited a successful switch to risperidone.6,11
